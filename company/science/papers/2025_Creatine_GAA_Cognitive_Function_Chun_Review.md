**Category**: **Limited Tested in Humans**

## Evidence Analysis

### Study Design Summary

This was a 6-week, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial comparing four groups: placebo, creatine monohydrate (10 g/day), guanidinoacetic acid (2 g/day), and combined creatine plus GAA. Researchers measured cognitive function using a battery of five validated tests assessing memory, reaction time, vigilance, and executive function before and after supplementation in 66 healthy, recreationally active adults.

### Research Question

Does high-dose creatine monohydrate supplementation (10 g/day), alone or combined with guanidinoacetic acid (2 g/day), improve cognitive function in healthy adults over a 6-week period?

---

### Key Findings (with Evidence)

#### Finding 1: CrM + GAA improved reaction times in Word Recognition test

The combination of creatine and GAA showed improvements in reaction time measures:

> "There was evidence that CrM + GAA improved overall reaction time (−18.1% [−34, −2], p = 0.028) and YES reaction time (−28.7% [−48, −10], p = 0.006) and tended to increase the percent of correct responses (4.4% [−0.8, 9.5], p = 0.093) while reducing correct reaction time (−15.3% [−33, −2.9], p = 0.098) compared to PLA"

Creatine alone also showed benefits:

> "YES reaction time (−29.5% [−50, −8.8], p = 0.006) was faster and correct NO responses tended to be higher with CrM (4.1% [−0.8, 9], p = 0.10) and CrM + GAA (4.8% [−0.5, 10.2], p = 0.074) compared to PLA in the Word Recognition test that assesses secondary memory through word familiarity and recall."

The Word Recognition test measures secondary memory, showing faster memory retrieval speed with supplementation.

#### Finding 2: CrM + GAA showed paradoxical improvements in Stroop test

The Stroop test measures executive function and cognitive control. The combination showed advantages over creatine alone:

> "In the Stroop test, which assesses cognitive function and executive control, there was evidence that CrM + GAA promoted faster overall reaction time (−18.1% [−34, −2], p = 0.028), correct reaction time (−11.8% [−21.5, −2.0], p = 0.018), incongruent reaction time (−19.9% [−34.3, −5.5], p = 0.008), and correct incongruent reaction time (−38.8% [−62.8, −14.9], p = 0.002) compared to the CrM group."

**However, a critical finding**:

> "However, no differences were observed compared to the PLA group in reaction time."

**NOTE**: This paradoxical result (CrM+GAA faster than CrM alone but not faster than placebo) indicates a complex system with unclear mechanisms. This finding does not support clear cognitive enhancement and raises questions about the reliability of these specific results.

#### Finding 3: Mixed results with speed-accuracy trade-off in Picture Recognition test

> "Similar to the Word Recognition test, there was evidence that overall reaction time on the Picture Recognition test tended to be faster with CrM + GAA compared to PLA (−8.2% [−18, 1.65], p = 0.10), although the percentage of correct responses was lower than PLA (−2.4% [−4.8, −.03], p = 0.048)."

**Important concern**: Faster responses with reduced accuracy suggests a speed-accuracy trade-off rather than genuine cognitive enhancement, potentially indicating impulsivity or reduced cognitive control.

#### Finding 4: No improvements in spatial/working memory (Corsi Block test)

> "No significant differences were observed in Corsi Block Span Score among groups (p = 0.36), which assesses present working and spatial memory."

Working memory and spatial memory showed no benefits from any supplementation.

#### Finding 5: Minimal improvements in sustained attention (Digit Vigilance test)

> "Finally, analysis of the Digit Vigilance test, which measures sustained attention and psychomotor speed, revealed that the percentage of correct responses was significantly higher in the CrM + GAA group compared to the GAA group (9.1% [1.6, 16.7], p = 0.019) with no differences observed among groups in the percentage of correct responses, correct response reaction time, or false alarm responses."

**Note**: This passage contains a contradiction - it states results were "significantly higher" but then "no differences observed." This reporting inconsistency raises concerns about result interpretation.

---

### Limitations of the Study (with Evidence)

1. **Small sample size with multiple groups**: With 66 participants divided into 4 groups, each group had only approximately 16-17 participants. This small per-group sample size severely limits statistical power and the reliability of findings.

2. **Positive effects primarily with CrM+GAA combination, not creatine alone**: Most significant improvements were seen with the combination therapy rather than creatine monohydrate alone, making it difficult to attribute benefits specifically to creatine. The GAA component appears essential for most observed effects.

3. **Lack of statistical correction for covariates**: The analysis methods described do not explicitly mention controlling for potential confounding variables such as age, sex, body weight, baseline cognitive performance, or education level. With 56% female participants and a wide age range (±14 years SD), these factors could significantly influence cognitive test performance.

4. **Inconsistent and paradoxical findings**: Several results don't follow logical patterns:
   - Stroop test: CrM+GAA was significantly faster than CrM alone but not significantly different from placebo
   - Picture Recognition: Faster responses but reduced accuracy (speed-accuracy trade-off)
   - Digit Vigilance: Contradictory reporting of results
   - These inconsistencies suggest complex interactions with unclear mechanisms or possible statistical artifacts.

5. **Speed-accuracy trade-offs question cognitive enhancement**: The Picture Recognition test showed faster reaction times but lower accuracy with CrM+GAA, suggesting participants may have responded more impulsively rather than with improved cognitive function.

6. **No improvement in key cognitive domains**: Important aspects of cognition showed no benefits:
   - Working memory and spatial memory (Corsi Block): no differences among groups
   - Sustained attention (Digit Vigilance): minimal and inconsistent effects

7. **Healthy, recreationally active population**: Participants were healthy adults (mean age 40.6 years) without cognitive impairment. Results may not generalize to populations with cognitive decline, older adults, or those with neurological conditions.

8. **Short intervention period**: 6 weeks may not be sufficient to observe maximal effects on brain creatine content or cognitive adaptations.

9. **Potential conflict of interest**: Alzchem Group, which manufactures and sells creatine and GAA supplements, provided the products, creating potential bias.

10. **Unclear test methodology**: The abstract does not fully explain cognitive test procedures (e.g., what "YES Reaction Time" specifically measures in context), limiting reproducibility assessment.

### Relationship to Claim

**Claim**: "Creatine helps brain function better"

**Paper's Stance**: Partially Supporting (with significant caveats)

**Alignment Assessment**:

The paper provides **limited and qualified support** for the claim with important limitations:

**Arguments for support:**
- The study does show some improvements in reaction time on memory tasks (Word Recognition) and executive function tasks (Stroop test), particularly with the CrM+GAA combination
- These improvements suggest faster cognitive processing in specific domains
- The study employed rigorous methodology (double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled)

**Arguments limiting support:**
1. **Effect requires GAA addition**: Most consistent positive effects were observed with CrM+GAA combination, not creatine monohydrate alone. The claim oversimplifies by focusing on "creatine" when GAA appears necessary for benefits.

2. **Selective improvements only**: Improvements were limited to reaction time measures. Key cognitive functions showed no improvement:
   - No improvement in working/spatial memory
   - No improvement in sustained attention
   - Mixed results with speed-accuracy trade-offs

3. **Paradoxical and inconsistent findings**: The Stroop test results (CrM+GAA better than CrM but not better than placebo) and contradictory Digit Vigilance reporting raise serious questions about result robustness and interpretation.

4. **Small sample size**: With only ~16-17 participants per group, reliability is limited and results could reflect individual variability or chance.

5. **Narrow population**: Tested only in healthy, recreationally active adults. The claim implies broader applicability than demonstrated.

**Conclusion**: The paper provides limited evidence that high-dose creatine combined with GAA may improve reaction times on specific cognitive tasks in healthy adults. The claim "Creatine helps brain function better" overstates the evidence by suggesting broader cognitive enhancement than supported, not distinguishing between creatine alone versus combination therapy, and not acknowledging selective nature of improvements and significant study limitations.

## Expert Comment (For Layperson Audience)

This 6-week study of 66 healthy adults (20 to 60 years old) found that creatine combined with GAA slightly improved reaction speed on one memory test but showed no improvement in working memory or attention. Creatine alone had minimal effect, and results were inconsistent across different cognitive tasks.

Classified as "Limited Tested in Humans" due to small sample size. Major concerns include lack of statistical correction for covariates (age, sex), contradictory findings, and conflict of interest (supplement manufacturer provided products). Evidence is insufficient to support broad claims that creatine improves brain function.

---

## Summary Assessment

**Key Takeaway**: While this well-designed study provides preliminary evidence that creatine combined with GAA may enhance reaction speed on specific memory tasks, the findings are limited by small sample size, lack of covariate adjustment, inconsistent results, and conflict of interest. The evidence does not support the broader claim that "creatine helps brain function better" in general, particularly as creatine alone showed minimal benefits and improvements were restricted to reaction time rather than overall cognitive enhancement.

**Recommendation for Evidence Decoded**: Classify as **Limited Tested in Humans - Supporting (with significant qualifications)** with emphasis on the combination therapy requirement and the selective nature of cognitive improvements.
