# PAPER REVIEW

## Publication Information
- **Title**: Low-Carbohydrate Training Increases Protein Requirements of Endurance Athletes
- **Authors**: Jenna B. Gillen, Daniel W. D. West, Eric P. Williamson, Hugo J. W. Fung, and Daniel R. Moore
- **Journal**: Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise (2019), Vol. 51, No. 11, pp. 2294–2301
- **DOI**: 10.1249/MSS.0000000000002036

---

## Claim Being Evaluated
**"Carbohydrates restriction make the body to burn amino acids for fuel instead of to build muscle"**

**Paper's Role**: Supporting paper

---

## Classification Workflow

### Step 1: Validation

- **Conflict of Interest**: No - The study was supported by grants from Ajinomoto Innovation Alliance Program, Canadian Foundations for Innovation, and Ontario Research Fund. While Ajinomoto is an amino acid manufacturer, the authors explicitly state "The authors report no conflicts of interest." The funding sources are academic/government grants, and the study design does not appear to favor any commercial interest.

- **Review Study**: No - This is original research with primary data collection using the indicator amino acid oxidation (IAAO) technique in a randomized crossover design.

- **Categorical Meta-Analysis**: No - This is an experimental study, not a meta-analysis.

- **Overstates Evidence**: Requires careful consideration for the specific claim being evaluated.

**Assessment of Overstates Evidence**: The paper shows that low-CHO availability increases amino acid oxidation by 11% and decreases net protein balance by 12% during recovery. However, the claim as stated is an oversimplification. The paper demonstrates:
1. Increased amino acid oxidation occurs with low-CHO training
2. This increases protein *requirements* to support protein synthesis
3. It does NOT show that protein synthesis stops or that muscle building is prevented entirely - rather that MORE protein is needed to achieve the same synthesis rates

The claim "instead of to build muscle" overstates the evidence. The study shows amino acids are used MORE for fuel, requiring higher protein intake to maintain synthesis - not that muscle building stops entirely.

**Result**: The claim partially overstates the evidence. If used as a **supporting** paper, this needs careful framing - the paper supports that low-CHO increases amino acid oxidation, but doesn't support that muscle building is completely replaced by amino acid burning.
