## Classification Workflow

### Step 1: Validation
- **Conflict of Interest**: YES - The study was supported by Mars Edge (provided infrastructure and study pills) and Pfizer Consumer Healthcare/Haleon (provided study pills). These are companies that could benefit from positive results about multivitamin supplements. However, the funding sources "had no role in the design and conduct of the study; collection, management, analysis, and interpretation of the data; preparation, review, or approval of the manuscript". Several authors received research support from Mars Edge. There is potential conflict of interest due to industry funding, though protections were in place.

- **Review Study**: NO - This is NOT a pure review paper. This is an original research study (randomized clinical trial) with original data collection. It includes a meta-analysis component, BUT the meta-analysis uses data from original studies within the same trial (COSMOS substudies). The study conducted new statistical analyses combining data from multiple substudies.

- **Categorical Meta-Analysis**: NO - The meta-analysis included quantitative data pooling with statistical models. Used random-effects models with summary effect estimates, confidence intervals, and P values. This is a quantitative meta-analysis, not categorical.

- **Overstates Evidence**: NO - The Claim is "Women need special 'women's multivitamins' with different formulations than men". What the paper shows: The study tested Centrum Silver (a standard multivitamin) on BOTH men and women together. The study found benefits for cognitive function with the SAME multivitamin formulation for both sexes. In subgroup analysis (Figure 2), there was NO significant interaction by sex (P-interaction = 0.88). This means men and women benefited similarly from the SAME multivitamin formulation. The paper actually provides evidence AGAINST the claim that women need different formulations than men, since both sexes benefited from the same formulation.

**Result**: Continue to Quality Assessment

### Step 2: Quality Assessment

**1. Study Design**: PASS
- Was the study designed to answer this claim? The study was designed to test the effect of MVM supplementation on cognitive function. While not specifically designed to compare men vs. women's formulations, the design allows us to evaluate whether the same formulation works for both sexes.
- Does the methodology directly address the question? Yes, the study included both men and women (49.2% female, 50.8% male) and tested subgroup effects by sex.
- Was it blinded? Yes - "randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled" design. All investigators, examiners, and participants were masked to treatment group assignment.
- Sample size for this assessment: 573 participants in COSMOS-Clinic; meta-analysis included 5,203 non-overlapping participants

**2. Control Group**: PASS
- Yes, there was a proper placebo control group
- Participants were randomly assigned to MVM (n=272) or placebo (n=301)
- Placebo pills were provided to ensure blinding
- The 2×2 factorial design also controlled for cocoa extract effects

**3. Bias Addressed**: PASS
- Confounding variables tracked: Age, sex, education, BMI, physical activity, diet quality (AHEI-2010), smoking, alcohol use, medical history (hypertension, CVD, diabetes, depression)
- Baseline characteristics: Comparable across treatment groups (Table 1)
- Models adjusted for: Age, sex, randomization to cocoa extract, and cocoa extract effect over time
- Randomization stratified by: Sex, age (5-year bins), and recruitment source

**4. Statistics**: PASS
- Used repeated measures models with unstructured covariance matrix
- Appropriate for longitudinal cognitive data
- Adjusted for multiple comparisons in meta-analysis (Bonferroni correction: P < 0.025 for two coprimary outcomes)
- Subgroup analyses used interaction terms to test for effect modification
- Maximum likelihood estimation with Wald tests
- Between-study heterogeneity assessed (τ² = 0%, P-heterogeneity > 0.60)

## Evidence Analysis

### Study Design Summary
This was a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial testing the effects of daily multivitamin-mineral (MVM) supplementation on cognitive function in 573 older adults (mean age 69.6 years, 49.2% female) over 2 years. The study used Centrum Silver, a standard broad-spectrum multivitamin containing 20+ vitamins and minerals. Participants underwent detailed in-person neuropsychological assessments at baseline and 2-year follow-up, measuring global cognition, episodic memory, and executive function. A meta-analysis combined results from three cognitive substudies totaling 5,203 non-overlapping participants to evaluate the strength of evidence for MVM effects on cognition.

### Research Question
What are the effects of daily multivitamin-mineral supplementation on cognitive function in older adults, and do these effects differ between men and women?

### Key Findings (with Evidence)

#### Finding 1: The same multivitamin formulation benefited both men and women equally

> "We did not observe significant interactions by selected subgroups for the effect of MVM supplementation compared with placebo on global cognitive change over 2 y" (Page 695, Results - Subgroup analyses)

From Figure 2 (Page 698), the subgroup analysis by sex shows:
- Female: Adjusted mean difference = 0.06 (95% CI: -0.03, 0.16)
- Male: Adjusted mean difference = 0.06 (95% CI: -0.03, 0.14)
- **P-interaction = 0.88**

This P-interaction of 0.88 indicates NO statistically significant difference in how men versus women responded to the multivitamin. Both sexes showed virtually identical benefit from the SAME formulation.

#### Finding 2: Both men and women were included and tested with the same Centrum Silver formulation

> "There were 272 participants randomly assigned to MVM, and 301 were randomly assigned to placebo. The mean (SD) age was 69.6 (5.3) y, females comprised 49.2%, and 37.9% reported prerandomization use of multivitamins" (Page 695, Table 1)

The study design section specifies:

> "COSMOS is a 2 × 2 factorial trial of cocoa extract (500 mg flavanols/d) and/or a daily MVM supplement for cardiovascular disease (CVD) and cancer prevention among 21,442 United States adults, aged ≥60 y" (Page 693, Methods)

The MVM supplement used was:

> "testing cocoa extract (CE; Mars Edge; 500 mg flavanols/d, including 80 mg (−)-epicatechin) and/or a daily MVM supplement (Haleon; Centrum Silver®)" (Page 693, Methods)

This confirms that both men and women received the SAME Centrum Silver formulation - not a gender-specific formulation.

#### Finding 3: The meta-analysis across all three substudies (5,203 participants) confirmed benefits for both sexes with the same formulation

> "The meta-analysis results for global cognition (Figure 3) show that participants randomly assigned to the MVM group compared with placebo had a statistically significant more favorable change in global cognition over time (mean difference (95% CI): 0.07 (0.03, 0.11) SU; P = 0.0009); findings remained significant after Bonferroni correction. The overall effect was not heterogeneous across studies (P-heterogeneity = 0.88), with no between-study variance (τ² = 0%)." (Page 697, Meta-analysis results)

For episodic memory:

> "The meta-analysis results for episodic memory (Figure 3) show that participants randomly assigned to the MVM group compared with placebo had a statistically significant more favorable change in episodic memory over time [mean difference (95% CI): 0.06 (0.03, 0.10) SU; P = 0.0007]; findings remained significant after Bonferroni correction." (Page 697, Meta-analysis results)

The clinical importance is stated as:

> "Regarding the clinical importance of our meta-analysis findings: the magnitude of the effect of MVM supplementation compared with placebo on global cognition score was equivalent on average to the difference observed between participants 2 y apart in age (that is, 2 y less cognitive aging)." (Page 697, Meta-analysis results)

#### Finding 4: The study population was well-characterized with balanced baseline characteristics

From Table 1 (Page 696), baseline characteristics were comparable between MVM and placebo groups:

Sex distribution:
- MVM group: Male 139 (51.1%), Female 133 (48.9%)
- Placebo group: Male 152 (50.5%), Female 149 (49.5%)

The paper notes:

> "Descriptive characteristics were comparable across treatment groups." (Page 695, COSMOS-clinic: baseline characteristics)

Regarding the broader COSMOS trial population:

> "COSMOS included both older males and females, whereas PHS II included only older males." (Page 697, Discussion)

This is important because it contrasts with previous studies that only included men. The fact that this study included nearly equal numbers of men and women and found no sex differences strengthens the evidence that gender-specific formulations are not necessary.

### Relationship to Claim
**Claim**: Women need special "women's multivitamins" with different formulations than men

**Paper's Stance**: Contradicting

**Alignment Assessment**: This paper directly contradicts the claim. The study tested the same multivitamin formulation (Centrum Silver) in both men and women and found no significant difference in treatment effect by sex (P-interaction = 0.88). Both sexes showed virtually identical cognitive benefits from the same standard multivitamin formulation, providing strong evidence that gender-specific formulations are not necessary for cognitive health benefits in older adults.

## Expert Comment (For Layperson Audience)

This randomized clinical trial tested whether the same multivitamin (Centrum Silver) benefits both men and women for brain health. 573 adults aged 60+ (282 women, 291 men) took either the multivitamin or placebo for 2 years with detailed cognitive testing. Results showed the same formulation improved memory equally in both sexes (P=0.88 for sex interaction). Since men and women benefited identically from the same multivitamin, this contradicts the claim that women need special "women's formulations."

**Recommendations**: Future studies should examine whether there are any sex-specific differences in micronutrient needs for other health outcomes beyond cognition, and test whether standard multivitamins provide equivalent benefits across diverse racial and ethnic populations.
