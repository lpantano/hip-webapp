## Final Classification

**Category**: **Inconclusive**

**Explanation**: While this meta-analysis pooled data from 8 randomized controlled trials with 225 healthy human participants and showed an overall positive effect (SMD = 0.29, P = 0.02), the study **fails the statistics quality criterion** due to:
1. High risk of bias (6 of 8 studies had poor randomization reporting)
2. Very high heterogeneity (I² = 66% overall)
3. The main positive signal comes from only 2 small studies in older adults (57 participants, I² = 83%)
4. Young adults showed no benefit (SMD = 0.03, P = 0.72)
5. Multiple confounders not addressed (dietary creatine intake, baseline brain creatine levels)
6. Different memory assessment tools combined, creating measurement inconsistency

The evidence is too weak and inconsistent to support confident conclusions about creatine's effects on memory.

## Evidence Analysis

### Study Design Summary

This is a systematic review and meta-analysis that pooled data from 8 randomized controlled trials examining creatine monohydrate supplementation versus placebo in healthy individuals. Researchers searched multiple databases (PubMed, Web of Science, Cochrane Library, Scopus) from inception to September 2021, identifying studies where healthy participants received creatine supplementation (doses 2.2–20 g/d for 5 days to 24 weeks) and were assessed on various memory performance measures. The meta-analysis used standardized mean differences to combine results across studies that used different memory assessment tools, applying a random-effects model to account for variability between studies.

### Research Question

Does creatine monohydrate supplementation improve memory performance in healthy individuals compared to placebo, and do factors such as age, sex, dose, or duration of supplementation influence these effects?

---

### Key Findings (with Evidence)

#### Finding 1: Overall Memory Improvement (Modest)

> "Overall, creatine supplementation improved measures of memory compared with placebo (standard mean difference [SMD] = 0.29, 95%CI, 0.04–0.53; I² = 66%; P = 0.02)." (Abstract, page 1)

The meta-analysis found a statistically significant but modest improvement in memory performance. This is shown in Figure 2 (forest plot) with 428 participants in creatine groups vs 419 in placebo groups across all memory outcomes. However, this came with moderate heterogeneity (I² = 66%), indicating substantial variability between studies. The standardized mean difference of 0.29 represents a small-to-medium effect size.

**Supporting context from Discussion:**
> "Results showed that measures of memory following creatine supplementation, compared with placebo, were improved. These benefits were more robust in older adults (66–76 years). However, a moderate risk of bias and significant heterogeneity between studies were observed, and therefore caution is warranted." (Page 7)

**Critical observation**: Looking at Figure 2, individual studies appear to drive the overall effect, suggesting high risk of bias from single studies rather than consistent evidence across the meta-analysis.

---

#### Finding 2: Strong Age-Dependent Effects (High Bias Risk)

> "Subgroup analyses revealed a significant improvement in memory in older adults (66–76 years) (SMD = 0.88; 95%CI, 0.22–1.55; I² = 83%; P = 0.009) compared with their younger counterparts (11–31 years) (SMD = 0.03; 95%CI, −0.14 to 0.20; I² = 0%; P = 0.72)." (Abstract, page 1)

This is the most striking finding: older adults showed a large effect (SMD = 0.88) while younger adults showed essentially no effect (SMD = 0.03, not statistically significant).

**Critical limitation**: Figure 3B shows the older adult subgroup consists of only **2 studies**:
- McMorris 2007b: 32 participants (68-85 years)
- Alves 2013: 25 participants (60-80 years)

**Total older adults: only 57 participants from 2 studies** with very high heterogeneity (I² = 83%), suggesting these two studies had conflicting results. This is an extremely weak evidence base for such a strong conclusion.

---

#### Finding 3: No Effect of Dose or Duration

> "Creatine dose (≈ 2.2–20 g/d), duration of intervention (5 days to 24 weeks), sex, or geographical origin did not influence the findings." (Abstract, page 1)

Subgroup analyses found neither dose nor duration reached statistical significance when analyzed separately:

**Dose:**
- Low dose (≤5 g/d): SMD = 0.24; 95%CI, −0.04 to 0.52; I² = 38%; P = 0.09 (not significant)
- High dose (>5 g/d): SMD = 0.33; 95%CI, −0.07 to 0.74; I² = 78%; P = 0.11 (not significant)

**Duration:**
- ≤2 weeks: SMD = 0.33; 95%CI, −0.07 to 0.74; I² = 78%; P = 0.11 (not significant)
- >2 weeks: SMD = 0.24; 95%CI, −0.04 to 0.52; I² = 38%; P = 0.09 (not significant)

**From the Discussion:**
> "This meta-analysis also reveals that supplementation with creatine monohydrate had no influence on memory performance following a higher dosing strategy (ie, > 5 g/d)... higher doses of creatine supplementation may not be required to optimize brain creatine content." (Page 8)

The authors acknowledge: "very little is known about the effect of the duration of the intervention" and "much-longer-term trials are required to show robust changes over time."

---

### Limitations of the Study (with Evidence)

#### 1. Small Sample Sizes and Limited Statistical Power

> "A limitation, however, is that the included studies did not assess baseline levels of serum or brain creatine. As such, it was not possible to determine whether non-responders had lower brain creatine levels than responders." (Page 9)

The total meta-analysis included only 225 participants across 8 studies. Individual studies ranged from 15 to 67 participants, severely limiting statistical power.

---

#### 2. High Heterogeneity and Multiple Memory Measures

> "There are also several methodological limitations, including the combination of various assessment tools designed to measure memory, which increases the heterogeneity of the data. In particular, several memory tests were combined to increase the statistical power (including short-term, long-term, and working memory)." (Page 9)

> "Moreover, the high diversity between the memory assessment tools could also influence the accuracy and lead to uncertainty of the effect estimate." (Page 10)

Different studies used completely different memory tests:
- Rey Auditory Verbal Learning Test (word list recall)
- Digit span test (forward/backward number sequences)
- Corsi block test (spatial memory)
- Running memory (letter matching task)
- Sternberg memory task (letter set recognition)
- Brief Battery of Cognitive Screening (composite, undefined)

This makes direct comparisons difficult and questionable.

---

#### 3. Moderate to High Risk of Bias

> "Risk of bias was scored as high in 6 RCTs because information about how randomization was applied was lacking. In 4 RCTs, some concerns were raised because of the absence of detail regarding treatment allocation... Finally, 8 RCTs had a study protocol that was not pre-specified, resulting in some concerns." (Page 7)

> "The included studies were of moderate quality, according to the Cochrane RoB 2 tool." (Page 9-10)

**Summary of bias issues:**
- 6 of 8 studies: High risk of bias (poor randomization reporting)
- 4 of 8 studies: Concerns about treatment allocation
- 8 of 8 studies: No pre-specified protocol
- Overall quality: Moderate (suboptimal)

---

#### 4. Dietary Creatine Intake Not Assessed

> "Furthermore, most of the included studies did not assess creatine intake from dietary consumption, which may have altered the findings." (Page 9)

This is particularly critical since:
- Vegetarians have lower baseline creatine levels
- One study (Benton & Donohoe 2011) found vegetarians responded better than omnivores
- Baseline creatine status likely affects response to supplementation

---

#### 5. No Assessment of Brain Creatine Levels

> "Additionally, the included studies did not assess baseline levels of serum or brain creatine." (Page 9)

**Critical problem**: Without measuring whether creatine actually reached the brain, it's unclear if:
- The mechanism of action is actually through increased brain creatine stores
- Non-responders simply didn't absorb or transport creatine to the brain
- The blood-brain barrier limited creatine uptake

One included study (Merege-Filho 2017) actually **failed to show increased brain creatine** despite supplementation, yet was still included in the meta-analysis.

---

#### 6. Limited Evidence in Older Adults (Where Effect Was Strongest)

The strongest effect was seen in older adults (SMD = 0.88, P = 0.009), but this is based on:
- Only **2 studies**
- Only **57 total participants**
- Very high heterogeneity (I² = 83%) indicating conflicting results

This is insufficient evidence to draw confident conclusions.

---

#### 7. Inability to Assess Publication Bias

> "Finally, it was not possible to assess publication bias, which could affect the quality of evidence, because the number of included studies was too low." (Page 10)

With only 8 studies, the authors could not perform funnel plot analysis or other publication bias assessments, meaning negative studies may be missing from the analysis.

---

#### 8. Conflicts of Interest

Two senior authors (Scott C. Forbes and Darren G. Candow) have significant financial ties to the creatine industry:
- Scientific advisor for creatine companies
- Industry-sponsored research
- Creatine donations for studies
- Travel support from creatine industry
- Scientific Advisory Board membership for AlzChem (creatine manufacturer)

While disclosed, these conflicts raise concerns about potential bias in study selection, analysis, or interpretation.

## Expert Comment (For Layperson Audience)

### Full Version

**Study Design**: This meta-analysis combined results from 8 randomized controlled trials involving 225 healthy people who took either creatine supplements (doses ranging from 2.2 to 20 grams per day) or placebo pills for periods ranging from 5 days to 24 weeks. Participants were then tested on various memory tasks including recalling lists of words or numbers, remembering spatial locations, and other cognitive tests. The researchers pooled the results to see if creatine improved memory performance overall.

**Main Findings**: The analysis found a small overall improvement in memory with creatine supplementation (standardized mean difference = 0.29, P = 0.02), but results varied greatly between studies (66% heterogeneity). The most interesting finding was that older adults (ages 66-76) showed a large memory benefit while younger adults (ages 11-31) showed no benefit at all. However, the older adult finding is based on only 2 small studies with just 57 people total, and those two studies had highly conflicting results (83% heterogeneity), making it unclear whether the effect is real or just statistical noise.

**Classification Rationale**: This study receives an **Inconclusive** classification because it fails the statistics quality criterion. Here's why: The overall effect is small and inconsistent across studies. The most promising finding—memory improvement in older adults—comes from only 2 studies with 57 participants and highly variable results between them. Six of the 8 studies had high risk of bias due to poor reporting of how participants were randomized. Different memory tests were combined together (word recall, number recall, spatial memory, etc.), making it unclear what aspect of memory, if any, actually improves. Most critically, the studies didn't measure whether creatine actually reached the brain—they just assumed it did. One study that did check found no increase in brain creatine despite supplementation. Dietary creatine intake (from meat and fish) wasn't assessed, which matters because vegetarians have lower baseline creatine and may respond differently than meat-eaters. The authors themselves state their findings "should be interpreted with caution" and call for better research.

**Alignment with Claim**: The claim states creatine helps "brain function" generally, but this paper only examined memory, which is just one aspect of brain function (others include attention, processing speed, executive function, mood, etc.). Even for the narrower claim about memory, the evidence is too weak and inconsistent to be confident. Young healthy adults saw no benefit whatsoever. Older adults may benefit, but that conclusion rests on extremely limited evidence (2 small studies with conflicting results). The claim substantially overstates what this research actually shows.

**Additional Concerns**: Two of the senior authors have significant financial conflicts of interest—one served as a scientific advisor for a creatine company, and another has conducted industry-sponsored creatine research, received free creatine for studies, received travel funding from the creatine industry, and serves on the Scientific Advisory Board for AlzChem, a company that manufactures creatine. While these conflicts were disclosed, they raise questions about potential bias in how the research was conducted or interpreted.

## Summary and Recommendations

### Critical Weaknesses
1. **Extremely limited evidence base** - Only 2 studies with 57 participants for the positive finding
2. **High risk of bias** - 6 of 8 studies had poor randomization
3. **High heterogeneity** - Results highly inconsistent between studies (I² = 66-83%)
4. **No mechanistic validation** - Brain creatine levels not measured
5. **Scope mismatch** - Claim is about "brain function" but only memory was tested
6. **Age-specific effects** - No benefit in young adults who make up majority of potential users
7. **Industry conflicts of interest** - Senior authors have financial ties to creatine manufacturers

### What Would Strengthen the Evidence

To properly evaluate whether creatine improves memory (let alone broader "brain function"), future research should:

1. **Larger sample sizes** - At least 200-500 participants per age group
2. **Direct brain measurements** - Use MRI spectroscopy to confirm creatine reaches the brain
3. **Standardized testing** - Use the same validated memory tests across all studies
4. **Longer follow-up** - 6-12 months minimum to assess sustained effects
5. **Baseline assessment** - Measure dietary creatine intake and baseline brain creatine levels
6. **Diverse populations** - Include different ethnicities, both sexes, vegetarians vs omnivores
7. **Multiple cognitive domains** - Test attention, processing speed, executive function, not just memory
8. **Independent funding** - Studies free from industry sponsorship
9. **Pre-registered protocols** - Publish study plans before starting to prevent selective reporting
10. **Head-to-head comparisons** - Compare different doses and durations systematically

### Bottom Line

The current evidence is **insufficient** to confidently claim that creatine improves memory in healthy adults, and certainly insufficient to claim it improves "brain function better" broadly. Young adults show no benefit. Older adults may benefit, but that conclusion rests on only 2 small, conflicting studies with high bias risk. The claim substantially overstates what the available evidence actually shows.
