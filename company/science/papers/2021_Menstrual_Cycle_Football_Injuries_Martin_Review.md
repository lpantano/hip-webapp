## Final Classification
**Category**: **INCONCLUSIVE**

**Explanation**: While this prospective study tracked 156 injuries across menstrual cycle phases in elite footballers, it has critical methodological limitations: (1) **No exposure data** - the study doesn't track how much time athletes spent training/competing in each phase, making it impossible to determine true injury risk; (2) **No inferential statistics** - only descriptive rates reported without confidence intervals or significance testing; (3) **Phase estimation** based on self-reported cycle length using regression equations rather than hormone measurements. These limitations prevent definitive conclusions about injury risk across the menstrual cycle.

## Evidence Analysis

### Study Design Summary

This prospective case-series study tracked injuries in England international footballers (U15-Senior level) over 4 years (2012-2016). When injuries occurred during training camps or matches, players self-reported their menstrual cycle information. Researchers used regression equations to estimate menstrual cycle phase based on reported cycle length: follicular phase (from menstruation to late follicular), late follicular phase (3 days around estimated ovulation when estrogen peaks), and luteal phase (after late follicular). They calculated injury incidence rates per 1,000 person-days for each phase.

### Research Question

Does injury incidence in elite female footballers vary across menstrual cycle phases, and does extended menstrual cycle length (being "overdue") influence injury risk?

### Key Findings (with Evidence)

**Finding 1: Injury rates varied across menstrual cycle phases**

> "Injury incidence rates per 1,000 person-days were 31.9 in the follicular, 46.8 in the late follicular, and 35.4 in the luteal phase, resulting in injury incidence rate ratios of 1.47 (Late follicular:Follicular), 1.11 (Luteal:Follicular), and 0.76 (Luteal:Late follicular)." (Abstract, page 1)

The **late follicular phase** showed 47% higher injury rates compared to follicular phase.

**Finding 2: Muscle and tendon injuries were elevated in late follicular phase**

> "Injury incident rate ratios showed that muscle and tendon injury rates were 88% greater in the late follicular phase compared to the follicular phase, with muscle rupture/tear/strain/cramps and tendon injuries/ruptures occurring over twice as often during the late follicular phase compared to other phases" (Abstract, page 1)

Specific rates from Table 1:
- Muscle/tendon injuries: Follicular=21.8, Late follicular=**40.9**, Luteal=21.7 per 1,000 person-days
- Injury rate ratio for muscle/tendon: Late follicular:Follicular = **1.88**

**Finding 3: 20% of injuries occurred when athletes were "overdue" menstruation**

> "20% of injuries were reported as occurring when athletes were 'overdue' menses." (Abstract, page 1)

> "Twenty eight of 142 (20%) injuries were reported as occurring after the regular cycle was expected to have resumed ('overdue'). Number of days over normal cycle length at time of injury ranged from 0 to 40 days (median 5 days)." (Results, page 3)

### Limitations of the Study

1. **No exposure data**: Critical limitation - training/match time per menstrual phase not tracked
   > "A more accurate assessment of risk could also be determined by knowing the exposure to training/matches within each menstrual cycle phase as an assumption of the data is that this exposure was even although this could not be determined." (Limitations, page 5)

2. **No objective hormone measurements**: Phases estimated from self-reported cycle length using regression equations
   > "Ideally, blood samples would be collected upon injury to confirm hormone concentrations and menstrual cycle phase, although this is less feasible in the type of large-scale project" (Limitations, page 5)

3. **Self-reported data with recall bias**:
   > "Self-reported menstrual cycle length was used to predict the time within the menstrual cycle that the injury occurred which can be inaccurate" (Limitations, page 5)

4. **No statistical testing**:
   > "Due to the nature of the study design and descriptive aims of the study, null hypothesis testing was not deemed appropriate." (Methods, page 3)

   No confidence intervals or p-values reported - impossible to determine if differences are statistically significant.

5. **Small numbers when stratified**: Only 16 injuries in late follicular phase; some injury types have very small counts (e.g., only 1 joint/ligament injury in late follicular)

6. **Non-independent observations**: Includes recurrent injuries from same athletes (27 players had multiple injuries, ranging 1-7 per player)

### Relationship to Claim

**Claim**: Injury risk is higher throughout the menstrual cycle

**Paper's Stance**: Mixed/Specific - Shows injury risk is **elevated specifically in the late follicular phase**, not uniformly throughout the cycle

**Alignment Assessment**:

The paper demonstrates that injury incidence **varies across** menstrual cycle phases, with the late follicular phase (around ovulation, when estrogen peaks) showing ~47% higher overall injury rates and 88% higher muscle/tendon injury rates compared to other phases. However:

- The claim "throughout the menstrual cycle" is **imprecise** - the data shows elevated risk in ONE specific phase (late follicular), not throughout all phases
- Follicular (31.9) and luteal (35.4) phases had similar, lower rates
- **Critical limitation**: Without exposure data (hours of training/competition per phase), we cannot determine if higher injury counts in late follicular phase reflect true increased risk or simply more exposure during that phase
- No statistical testing means differences could be due to chance
- Relatively small sample when broken down by phase and injury type

The paper provides **suggestive but inconclusive evidence** that muscle/tendon injury risk may be elevated during the late follicular phase (around ovulation) in elite female footballers.

## Expert Comment (For Layperson Audience)

This study tracked injuries in elite England women's football players over 4 years to see if injury rates varied across the menstrual cycle. Researchers found that muscle and tendon injuries occurred nearly twice as often during the "late follicular phase" - the 3 days around ovulation when estrogen levels peak. They also noted that 20% of injuries happened when athletes were "overdue" for their period.

However, this study has major limitations that prevent us from concluding injury risk is truly higher during certain menstrual phases. The biggest problem: **researchers didn't track how much time athletes spent training or playing during each phase**. If athletes happened to train harder or compete more during the late follicular phase, more injuries would occur simply from more exposure - not because that phase is inherently riskier. Additionally, the study used only descriptive statistics without testing whether the observed differences could be due to chance. Menstrual phases were estimated from self-reported cycle length rather than measured with blood hormone tests.

**Classification: Inconclusive** - While this exploratory study suggests a pattern worth investigating further, the lack of exposure data and statistical testing means we cannot determine if injury risk actually increases during specific menstrual cycle phases.

**Recommendations**: Future research should track training/match exposure hours during each menstrual phase, use hormone measurements to confirm cycle phases, include statistical testing with confidence intervals, and study larger sample sizes to strengthen evidence.
