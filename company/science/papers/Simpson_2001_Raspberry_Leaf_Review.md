### Step 1: Validation

- **Conflict of Interest**: No - The study was partially funded by the New South Wales Nurses Registration Board Scholarship. Tablets were supplied by Blackmores Naturopathics, but this appears to be material support rather than industry-funded research with financial ties. The authors are midwives and academics with no disclosed financial conflicts.

- **Review Study**: No - This is original research, a double-blind randomized placebo-controlled trial.

- **Categorical Meta-Analysis**: No - This is a primary clinical trial, not a meta-analysis.

- **Overstates Evidence**: No - The authors are appropriately cautious in their conclusions, acknowledging limitations and the need for further research.

**Result**: Continue to Quality Assessment

### Step 2: Quality Assessment

- **Study Design**: PASS - This was a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial, which is the gold standard for clinical research. Women were randomly allocated to treatment (n=96) or placebo (n=96) groups. The study was blinded - tablets were identical in appearance.

> "This was a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial to explore the effect of the raspberry leaf herb, consumed in tablet form by nulliparous women from 32 weeks' gestation until the commencement of labor, on labor and birth outcomes." (page 52)

- **Control Group**: PASS - Proper placebo control group was used. Both tablets contained the same base ingredients (calcium phosphate, cellulose microcrystalline, magnesium stearate, soy polysaccharide), with the only difference being the addition of 1.2g raspberry leaf extract to treatment tablets.

> "The constituents of both types of tablets (raspberry leaf and placebo) were calcium phosphate, cellulose microcrystalline, magnesium stearate, and soy polysaccharide. The only difference was the addition of .4 g (3:1 = 1.2-g extract) of raspberry leaf to the raspberry leaf tablets." (page 53)

- **Bias Addressed**: PASS - Groups were compared for baseline characteristics (age, weight, ethnicity). The randomization process distributed confounders between groups. The study tracked multiple safety and outcome variables.

> "The treatment and control groups were very similar in the distribution of maternal weight, age, and ethnicity." (page 57)

> "There was no difference between the two groups for age [t(190) = .65, p = .52] and weight [t(190) = .13, p = .90]." (page 54)

- **Statistics**: PASS - Appropriate statistical tests were used: t-tests for continuous variables, chi-square tests for categorical variables, analysis of covariance for blood pressure comparisons. Sample size was calculated a priori with power analysis (α=0.05, power=0.80).

> "Thus, with a significance level set at .05 and power set at .80, the required sample size would be 100." (page 53)

**Result**: Continue to Study System

### Step 3: Study System

- **System Used**: Human Studies

This was a clinical trial conducted in pregnant women at a tertiary-level hospital in Sydney, Australia. Participants were low-risk, nulliparous women who birthed between May 1999 and February 2000.

> "The sample consisted of 192 low-risk, nulliparous women who birthed their babies between May 1999 and February 2000 at a large tertiary-level hospital in Sydney, Australia." (Abstract, page 51)

**Result**: Continue to Study Type

### Step 4: Study Type

- **Type**: Clinical Trial (Randomized Controlled Trial)

This was an interventional study where participants were actively assigned to receive either raspberry leaf tablets or placebo from 32 weeks gestation until labor onset.

> "This was a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial to explore the effect of the raspberry leaf herb, consumed in tablet form by nulliparous women from 32 weeks' gestation until the commencement of labor, on labor and birth outcomes." (page 52)

**Intervention details**:
- Treatment: 2 × 1.2g raspberry leaf extract tablets per day (2.4g total daily)
- Control: Identical placebo tablets
- Duration: From 32 weeks gestation until labor onset
- Administration: One tablet with breakfast, one with evening meal

### Step 5: Sample Size

- **Participants**: 192 (96 treatment, 96 placebo)
- **Sample Size Category**: 100-500,000

> "The total sample size was 192, which consisted of 96 women in the group who ingested raspberry leaf tablets (RL) and 96 in the placebo group (P)." (page 54)

Note on dropout: Originally 240 women were recruited, with 48 withdrawals (20% dropout rate). Withdrawals were evenly distributed between groups and reasons included nausea (6), diarrhea (2), constipation (1), medical reasons (hypertension, polyhydramnios, twin pregnancy), and non-specific reasons.

## Final Classification

**Category**: **Tested in Humans**

**Explanation**: This study passes all validation checks and quality assessments. It is a well-designed double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial conducted in 192 human participants (between 100-500,000 threshold). The study directly examined the claim about raspberry leaf's effects on labor.

## Evidence Analysis

### Study Design Summary

Researchers conducted a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial at a tertiary hospital in Sydney, Australia. Low-risk, nulliparous (first pregnancy) women were randomly assigned to receive either raspberry leaf tablets (2.4g/day) or identical placebo tablets from 32 weeks gestation until labor onset. The study measured labor duration, mode of birth, medical interventions, and safety outcomes for mother and baby.

### Research Question

Does regular intake of raspberry leaf tablets from 32 weeks gestation by nulliparous women:
1. Have any adverse effects on mother or baby?
2. Affect the duration of pregnancy (preterm or postterm)?
3. Shorten the duration of labor?
4. Reduce the likelihood of medical interventions during labor and birth?

### Key Findings (with Evidence)

#### Finding 1: No effect on first stage of labor duration

The primary outcome - shortening of labor - was NOT demonstrated for the first stage of labor. Both groups had nearly identical first stage durations.

> "No difference in first stage (determined by period between cervical dilatation of 3 cm accompanied by regular contractions to full dilatation)... was found between the two groups" (page 56)

| Outcome | Raspberry Leaf Group | Placebo Group | p-value |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|---------|
| First stage (minutes) | 428.2 ± 227.7 | 427.4 ± 209.5 | 0.98 |

#### Finding 2: Modest shortening of second stage of labor (not statistically significant)

The raspberry leaf group had a shorter second stage of labor by approximately 9.6 minutes, but this difference was NOT statistically significant.

> "The mean difference between the groups for the period of second stage (period between full dilatation of the cervix and birth of the baby) of labor was 9.6 minutes (the raspberry leaf group was 12% shorter in time on average than the placebo group), [t(146) = 1.08, p = .28]." (page 56)

| Outcome | Raspberry Leaf Group | Placebo Group | p-value |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|---------|
| Second stage (minutes) | 71.2 ± 48.9 | 80.8 ± 59.0 | 0.28 |

The authors describe this as "clinically significant" but it failed to reach statistical significance (p = 0.28).

#### Finding 3: No effect on labor induction or gestation length

Raspberry leaf did NOT induce labor earlier or affect the duration of pregnancy.

> "On average, the length of gestation in days for each group was the same [MRL = 279.69 and MP = 280.47, t(190) = .66, p = .51]." (page 56)

> "There was no difference between the two groups on the likelihood of medical augmentation of labor [χ²(2) = .18, p = .91]." (page 56)

| Outcome | Raspberry Leaf Group | Placebo Group | p-value |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|---------|
| Gestation (days) | 279.69 | 280.47 | 0.51 |
| Spontaneous labor | 48.4% | 50.6% | NS |
| Required induction | 20.4% | 21.4% | NS |

#### Finding 4: Lower rate of forceps deliveries (not statistically significant)

Women in the raspberry leaf group had a lower rate of forceps/vacuum-assisted deliveries compared to placebo, but this difference did NOT reach statistical significance.

> "The analyses suggested that women in the raspberry leaf group were more likely to give birth to their baby vaginally and unassisted and less likely to require a forceps delivery compared with the control group. Although not statistically significant, this result is considered to be clinically significant and may be associated with the reduction in the length of the second stage of labor." (page 57)

| Mode of Birth | Raspberry Leaf Group (n=93) | Placebo Group (n=89) |
|---------------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| Vaginal (cephalic) | 53 (57%) | 44 (49.4%) |
| Vaginal (breech) | 5 (5.4%) | 1 (1.1%) |
| Forceps/vacuum | 18 (19.3%) | 27 (30.4%) |
| Emergency cesarean | 17 (18.3%) | 17 (19.1%) |

> "Analysis of the data relating to mode of birth showed some difference between the groups [χ²(2) = 3.35, p = .18]." (page 56)

#### Finding 5: No adverse effects on mother or baby

The study found no evidence of harm from raspberry leaf consumption.

> "Raspberry leaf, consumed in tablet form, was found to cause no adverse effects for mother or baby" (Abstract, page 51)

**Maternal safety:**
> "No significant difference between the groups was demonstrated [MRL = 345.5 mL, MP = 357.2 mL, t(147) = .18, p = .86]." (blood loss, page 55)

> "No difference was found between the two groups in this analysis of covariance." (blood pressure, page 55)

**Neonatal safety:**
> "Analysis of birth weight also showed no significant difference between the two groups [MRL = 3456.15 g, MP = 3,500.42 g, t(190) = .74, p = .46]." (page 55)

| Safety Outcome | Raspberry Leaf | Placebo | p-value |
|----------------|---------------|---------|---------|
| Blood loss (mL) | 345.5 | 357.2 | 0.86 |
| Birth weight (g) | 3456.15 | 3500.42 | 0.46 |
| Apgar at 5 min | 8.81 | 8.95 | 0.108 |
| NICU admissions | 10 (5.2%) | 7 (3.7%) | NS |

### Limitations of the Study (with Evidence)

**1. Sub-therapeutic dosage used**

The authors acknowledge using a conservative dose that may have been too low to detect an effect. The recommended dose is 4-8g/day, but this study used only 2.4g/day.

> "Because this was the first study of its kind, a conservative dose (2.4 g per day) was chosen on the basis of the dosage recommended by the Pharmaceutical Society of Great Britain (4–8 g per day)" (page 53)

> "It seems that a daily intake of 2.4 g of raspberry leaf in tablet form (as was used for this study) was enough to produce some positive effects... while maintaining safety for mother and baby. The question now remains whether a higher dosage or commencement of the tablets at an earlier gestational age would contribute to a more clinically significant outcome." (page 58)

**2. Limited population diversity**

The sample was predominantly Caucasian women from one hospital in Australia, limiting generalizability.

> "The sample consisted of a majority of Caucasian women." (page 54)

| Ethnicity | Raspberry Leaf Group | Placebo Group |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------|
| Caucasian | 81 (84.4%) | 79 (82.3%) |
| Asian | 12 (12.5%) | 15 (15.6%) |
| South Pacific Islander | 3 (3.1%) | 2 (2.1%) |

**3. Nulliparous women only**

The study excluded multiparous women, so findings may not apply to women who have previously given birth.

> "Criteria for selection in the study included being nulliparous with a low-risk, healthy pregnancy" (page 53)

**4. Late initiation of treatment**

Tablets were only started at 32 weeks gestation, which may not have allowed sufficient time for the herb to exert effects.

> "The question now remains whether a higher dosage or commencement of the tablets at an earlier gestational age would contribute to a more clinically significant outcome." (page 58)

**5. Self-reported compliance**

Tablet consumption was self-reported, introducing potential for inaccuracy.

> "Because tablet consumption was self-reported by participants, it can only be assumed that the information provided to the authors was accurate." (page 58)

**6. Baseline imbalance in private care**

More women in the raspberry leaf group received private obstetric care, which could influence interventions.

> "The only notable difference between the two groups was that there were more women in the raspberry leaf group who chose to receive their care from a private obstetrician (11/11.5%) compared with the control group (5/5.2%)." (page 54)

**7. Study underpowered for secondary outcomes**

The sample size was calculated to detect a 2-hour difference in labor length, and may have been insufficient to detect smaller but meaningful differences.

### Relationship to Claim

**Claim**: "Red raspberry leaf tea shortens labor and helps induce birth"

**Paper's Stance**: Contradicting / Does not support the claim

**Alignment Assessment**: This study does NOT support the claim. The RCT found:
- No effect on first stage of labor (p=0.98)
- No statistically significant effect on second stage (p=0.28)
- No effect on gestation length or labor induction (p=0.51)
- No difference in rates of spontaneous vs induced labor

The study provides evidence that raspberry leaf, at least at the dose tested (2.4g/day from 32 weeks), does not shorten labor or induce birth.

## Expert Comment (For Layperson Audience) - Short Version

**Study Design**: Double-blind RCT with 192 first-time pregnant women in Australia comparing raspberry leaf tablets (2.4g/day) to placebo from 32 weeks until labor.

**Main Findings**: No statistically significant effects found. First stage labor was identical between groups. Second stage was ~10 minutes shorter in raspberry leaf group (p=0.28, not significant). No effect on inducing labor - gestation length was the same. Lower forceps rate (19.3% vs 30.4%) but not statistically significant.

**Classification Rationale**: Classified as "Tested in Humans" due to proper RCT design with adequate sample size. Main limitation: used half the recommended dose (2.4g vs 4-8g recommended).

**Alignment with Claim**: This study does NOT support the claim that raspberry leaf shortens labor or helps induce birth. No significant differences were found for labor duration or induction rates.

**Recommendation**: Larger trials using full therapeutic doses (4-8g/day) started earlier in pregnancy, with diverse populations including multiparous women, are needed to definitively evaluate this claim.
