## Final Classification
**Category**: **MISINFORMATION**

**Explanation**: This paper is classified as misinformation because the claim "injury risk is higher throughout the menstrual cycle" significantly overstates what the research actually found. The study reported **no statistically significant differences** in injury incidence or injury burden when comparing the three phases of the menstrual cycle (follicular, ovulatory, and luteal phases). The paper's own conclusion states it "reduces its possible relationship or influence on the injury distribution during each phase of the MC." While the authors observed some clinical trends (higher injury burden in follicular phase: 30.63 days-off vs 23.6 ovulatory and 17.59 luteal), these differences were not statistically significant and do not support the claim that injury risk is definitively higher throughout the menstrual cycle.

## Evidence Analysis

### Study Design Summary
This was a prospective cohort study conducted over two consecutive seasons (2017-2018 and 2018-2019) with fourteen professional female futsal teams from Spanish First (11 teams) and Second National Futsal (3 teams) leagues. The study tracked injury occurrence and menstrual cycle phases simultaneously. Players self-tracked their menstrual cycles using calendar-based counting (Google Calendar) or apps, verified by physical coaches. The menstrual cycle was divided into three phases: follicular (days 1-12), ovulatory (days 13-15), and luteal (days 16-28), based on a standard 28-day cycle. Injuries were defined as events during training or matches that caused the player to miss the next session/match.

### Research Question
Does the phase of the menstrual cycle (follicular, ovulatory, or luteal) affect the incidence, burden, and characteristics of injuries in elite female futsal players?

### Key Findings (with Evidence)

**Finding 1: No significant differences in injury incidence across menstrual cycle phases**

> "Regarding the comparison among different phases of MC, no statistical differences were observed on injury incidence neither injury burden between them (Table 2) (p > 0.05 in all comparisons)." (Page 3, Results section)

> "No statistical differences (p > 0.05) were reported for any variable comparing among the three phases of MC." (Page 1, Abstract)

The overall injury incidence was 5.68 injuries/1000 hours (95% CI: 5.41-5.96). When broken down by phase:
- Follicular: 5.64 injuries/1000h (95% CI: 5.24-6.04)
- Ovulatory: 5.59 injuries/1000h (95% CI: 4.95-6.23)
- Luteal: 5.79 injuries/1000h (95% CI: 5.29-6.29)

Statistical testing showed p > 0.05 for all comparisons, indicating no significant differences.

**Finding 2: Clinical (non-significant) trend toward higher injury burden in follicular phase**

> "However, clinical differences were observed among the three phases, with follicular phase showing the highest time lost by an injury (30.63 days-off) compared with ovulatory and luteal phases, with 23.60 and 17.59 days-off, respectively." (Page 3, Results section)

While this shows a numerical difference in recovery time, the authors explicitly state these differences were not statistically significant (p > 0.05).

**Finding 3: No significant differences in injury location, type, or temporal distribution across cycle phases**

> "Regarding the comparison among phases, there were no statistical differences on the distribution of injuries among follicular, ovulatory and luteal phases (p > 0.179) for all anatomy locations." (Page 3, Results section)

> "Regarding phases of the MC, no differences between phases was reported for three factors (p: 0.424, 0.161 and 0.380, respectively)." (Page 3, Results section, referring to moment of session, match-day approach, and quarter of season)

The most common injuries were quadriceps (22.0%) and ankle (20.4%) strains and sprains, but their distribution across menstrual phases showed no statistical significance.

**Finding 4: Study conclusion explicitly states it does NOT support a strong relationship**

> "This study suggests the relevance to track the MC, but reduces its possible relationship or influence on the injury distribution during each phase of the MC." (Page 1, Abstract)

> "To sum up, these findings are relevant for physical coaches to understand the relevance to monitor the menstrual cycle on their athletes and, also, educate them to know in deep their side effects on each phase..." (Page 6, Conclusions)

The authors acknowledge their findings do not demonstrate the relationship suggested by the claim.

### Limitations of the Study (with Evidence)

**Limitation 1: Menstrual cycle phases were self-reported, not objectively verified**

> "First of all, the MC phases were recorded without the control of hormone concentrations, which should be the most objective method (Janse DE Jonge, Thompson, & Han, 2019). Nevertheless, recent studies have accepted this limitation in cohort studies..." (Page 5, Discussion)

Self-reported cycle tracking using apps lacks the precision of hormone measurement (blood tests, ovulation kits), which could lead to misclassification of cycle phases.

**Limitation 2: Hormonal contraceptive use not controlled in first season**

> "Second, during the first season, the use of HC was not controlled. In this sense, the second season reported about a 5% of players using HC (which were discarded for this study)..." (Page 5, Discussion)

This means some first-season data may have included players whose natural menstrual cycles were altered by hormonal contraceptives.

**Limitation 3: Indirect calculation of exposure time**

> "Finally, the exposure time was calculated indirectly, but this bias has been also accepted due to the trouble and logistic to register this individual data per player in different teams (Stovitz & Shrier, 2012)." (Page 6, Discussion)

Individual player exposure time was not tracked precisely, which could introduce error in calculating injury rates.

**Limitation 4: Limited sample size and generalizability**

The study included 179 player-seasons with only 191 total injuries across two full seasons. This relatively small number of injury events may have limited statistical power to detect true differences. Additionally, the study only examined elite futsal players from Spain, limiting generalizability to other sports, competitive levels, or populations.

**Limitation 5: Variability in menstrual cycle length not fully accounted for**

> "Taking into account the variability on the length of MC in athletes, each player was encouraged to calculate their three main phases regarding their mean length of MC..." (Page 3, Methods)

While players were asked to adjust for their own cycle length, the standardized 28-day division (follicular: 1-12, ovulatory: 13-15, luteal: 16-28) may not have accurately reflected individual variations, particularly in athletes who often have irregular cycles.

### Relationship to Claim

**Claim**: Injury risk is higher throughout the menstrual cycle

**Paper's Stance**: **Contradicting**

**Alignment Assessment**: This paper directly contradicts the claim. The research found no statistically significant evidence that menstrual cycle phase affects injury risk in elite female futsal players. All comparisons across the three menstrual phases (follicular, ovulatory, luteal) showed p > 0.05, meaning the differences observed could easily be due to random chance rather than a true relationship with menstrual cycle phase.

While the paper's title suggests "follicular phase is related to higher tendency to suffer from severe injuries," the actual data does not support this statement with statistical significance. The authors' own conclusion explicitly states the study "reduces its possible relationship" between menstrual cycle and injury distribution.

The claim being evaluated is even broader, suggesting injury risk is "higher throughout the menstrual cycle" (implying all phases or the cycle in general increases risk), which is not supported at all by this paper. The paper examined whether risk *varies between phases*, not whether having a menstrual cycle increases baseline risk. Even on the narrower question of phase variation, the paper found no significant evidence.

Using this paper to support the claim represents a misinterpretation of the research findings and constitutes misinformation.

## Expert Comment (For Layperson Audience)

**Study Design**: This was a prospective cohort study that followed 179 elite female futsal players from Spain over two consecutive seasons (2017-2019). Researchers tracked all injuries that occurred during training and matches, recording when they happened in relation to each player's menstrual cycle. Players self-tracked their cycles using calendar apps, dividing the cycle into three phases: follicular (days 1-12), ovulatory (days 13-15), and luteal (days 16-28). The researchers then compared injury rates across these three phases to see if certain times in the cycle were riskier than others.

**Main Findings**: The study recorded 191 total injuries across both seasons. When researchers compared injury rates between the different phases of the menstrual cycle, they found no statistically significant differences. The overall injury incidence was 5.68 injuries per 1,000 hours of play, with rates of 5.64 during follicular phase, 5.59 during ovulatory phase, and 5.79 during luteal phase (all differences p > 0.05, meaning not statistically significant). While players who got injured during the follicular phase missed slightly more days on average (30.63 days) compared to the ovulatory phase (23.6 days) or luteal phase (17.59 days), this difference was also not statistically significant. The types of injuries (strains, sprains), locations (quadriceps, ankle, knee), and timing within training sessions showed no meaningful differences across menstrual phases. The authors concluded that their study "reduces its possible relationship or influence on the injury distribution during each phase of the MC."

**Classification Rationale**: This paper is classified as misinformation when used to support the claim that "injury risk is higher throughout the menstrual cycle." The claim suggests that the menstrual cycle definitively increases injury risk, but this paper found the opposite: no significant evidence that menstrual cycle phase affects injury occurrence or severity. The paper's title emphasizes a "higher tendency" in the follicular phase, creating an impression that may mislead readers, but the actual statistical analysis shows no significant relationship. All comparisons between menstrual phases yielded p-values greater than 0.05, which in scientific terms means the observed differences could easily be due to random chance rather than a true effect of menstrual cycle phase. The authors themselves acknowledge their results do not demonstrate a clear relationship between menstrual cycle phase and injury distribution. Using this paper to support the claim that menstrual cycle increases injury risk fundamentally misrepresents the research findings.

**Alignment with Claim**: The paper does not support the claim and actually provides evidence against it. The claim states injury risk is "higher throughout the menstrual cycle," but this study found no evidence of elevated risk during any particular phase or throughout the cycle in general. Every statistical comparison failed to reach significance (p > 0.05). While the paper's title and some discussion points mention a "tendency" toward more severe injuries in the follicular phase, this is based on non-significant clinical observations rather than statistically validated findings. The disconnect between the paper's title and its actual results highlights the importance of reading beyond abstracts and titles to understand what a study truly demonstrates. In this case, the data clearly shows no relationship between menstrual cycle phase and injury risk in elite female futsal players.

**Recommendations**: To properly investigate whether menstrual cycle affects injury risk in female athletes, future research would need several improvements. First, objective hormone measurement through blood tests or ovulation prediction kits should replace self-reported cycle tracking to ensure accurate phase classification. Second, much larger sample sizes with more injury events are needed to provide sufficient statistical power to detect true differences if they exist—191 injuries over two seasons across 179 players may not be enough. Third, studies should include diverse populations across multiple sports, competitive levels, and countries to understand if findings are generalizable or sport-specific. Fourth, individual player exposure time should be tracked precisely rather than estimated, ensuring accurate calculation of injury rates. Fifth, important confounding factors like training load, fatigue levels, sleep quality, nutrition status, and previous injury history should be measured and controlled for in analyses. Finally, studies should examine whether menstrual cycle irregularities, which are common in elite athletes, might themselves be a risk factor independent of cycle phase. Until such rigorous studies are conducted and show consistent findings, claims that the menstrual cycle increases injury risk remain unsupported by strong scientific evidence. This particular paper actually contributes evidence suggesting menstrual cycle phase does not significantly affect injury risk, at least in elite futsal players.
